\title{Calibrated prediction-horizon bounds and the measured
predictability floor of learned forecasters}

\author{DEX-zha}
\homepage{https://github.com/DEX-zha/ARSAC-Horizon-Experiment}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher}

\date{July 6, 2026}

\begin{abstract}
A point forecast does not say for how long it can be trusted. We study the
prediction horizon of an arbitrary forecaster, defined as the number of
rollout steps before its error first exceeds a tolerance $\tau$, and build
an instrument that turns this quantity into a calibrated, per-instant
guarantee. First, we define right-censored horizon labels and construct a
conformal lower bound $L(x)$ with target coverage
$\mathbb{P}(\Hw \ge L(x)) \ge 1-\alpha$; we prove that right-censoring can
only make the bound more conservative, and we complement it with a
per-window certified bound obtained from Lipschitz constants. Second, we
introduce a paired twin-experiment protocol that measures, rather than
assumes, whether a learned forecaster has reached the physical
predictability floor of a chaotic system. On Lorenz-63, a degree-6
polynomial next-generation reservoir computer attains a pre-registered
floor criterion: $\rho = H_{\mathrm{model}}/H_{\mathrm{twin}} = 0.834$ and
$0.862$ on two seeds, 18 to 19 Lyapunov times of valid prediction, and
error growth at $1.15$ to $1.21$ times the Lyapunov rate. The protocol
yields an injection-floor law, $H_{\mathrm{inj}} \approx
\ln(\tau\lambda_1\,dt/e_0)/(\lambda_1 dt)$, derived and then verified to
within 12 to 21\% on two systems, and maps a functional generality
boundary: R\"ossler is near-floor, while Mackey--Glass remains
model-limited because a polynomial family cannot capture the Jacobian of
its Hill nonlinearity. Third, the same error-accumulation framework
predicts a qualitatively different horizon law in the stable
($\lambda \to 0$) limit, $H(\tau) \approx (\tau/\sigma_\eff)^s$ with
$s \in [1,2]$, whose scale $\sigma_\eff$ is predicted with no free
parameter by an independent one-step noise estimator. A pre-registered
validation on hourly load from two independent balancing regions of the
U.S.\ grid passes all 17 of 17 criteria: the power law holds at
$R^2 > 0.99$ with the chaotic signature rejected, $\sigma_\eff$ is
predicted to within 10 to 12\%, conformal coverage holds 6 out of 6 times
with serial-dependence-aware bootstrap lower bounds, and the operational
decision replicates out of period at 97\% stability. Every number in this
paper is regenerated by versioned scripts, and the evidence files are
pinned by an automated test suite.
\end{abstract}

\maketitle

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Every operational forecast is eventually wrong. What a practitioner needs
is not only the forecast but the answer to a second question: for how many
steps ahead can this particular forecaster, from this particular initial
condition, be trusted within a stated tolerance? Standard uncertainty
quantification answers a per-step question, namely an interval around
$\hat y_{t+h}$ for each $h$. It does not directly deliver the object a
scheduler or an operator consumes, which is a guaranteed usable horizon.

This paper develops and validates an instrument for that object, and uses
it to answer a scientific question along the way. Our contributions are
the following.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A calibrated horizon bound} (Sec.~\ref{sec:framework}).
  We define per-window, right-censored horizon labels $\Hw$ and construct a
  conformal lower bound $L(x)$ targeting $\mathbb{P}(\Hw \ge L(x)) \ge
  1-\alpha$. We prove that right-censoring is conservative for a lower
  bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:censoring}), give an identification guard
  for the saturated regime, quantify the serial-dependence caveat
  empirically (calibration thinning leaves coverage unchanged; the
  residual one-point deficit is a trajectory-level shift with a validated
  $\alpha$-margin remedy), and complement the statistical bound with a
  per-window certified bound from Lipschitz constants
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:certified}).

  \item \textbf{A measured predictability floor} (Sec.~\ref{sec:floor}).
  We introduce a paired twin-experiment protocol that operationalizes the
  claim ``this forecaster has exhausted the predictive content of the
  system'': for each test window, the \emph{true} dynamics is launched
  from the exact state perturbed at the model's own one-step error $e_0$,
  and the model's horizon is compared to the twin's under identical
  criteria, with pre-registered thresholds and positive controls. A
  degree-6 next-generation reservoir computer (NG-RC) on the full Lorenz
  state reaches the floor: $\rho = 0.834/0.862$ (criterion $\ge 0.8$, two
  seeds), 18 to 19 Lyapunov times of valid prediction, and effective error
  growth at $1.15$ to $1.21\times\lambda_1$. We derive and verify an
  injection-floor law for step-by-step predictors and map the boundary of
  the result across systems; the boundary turns out to be functional (the
  model family's ability to represent the Jacobian), not dimensional.

  \item \textbf{A horizon scaling law for the stable limit, validated
  pre-registered on industrial data} (Secs.~\ref{sec:stable}
  and~\ref{sec:industrial}). The same error-accumulation framework
  predicts $H(\tau) \approx \ln(\tau/e_0)/(\lambda_1 dt)$ in the chaotic
  regime but $H(\tau) \approx (\tau/\sigma_\eff)^s$, $s \in [1,2]$, in the
  stable limit, with the scale $\sigma_\eff$ predicted by an independent
  noise estimator. On hourly electric load from two independent balancing
  regions (AEP and PJM-East), all 17 pre-registered checks pass: regime
  classification, power-law fit at $R^2 > 0.99$ with the logarithmic
  (chaotic) signature rejected, scale predicted to within 10 to 12\%,
  coverage held 6 out of 6 with circular-block-bootstrap lower bounds, and
  out-of-period decision replication at 97\%.

  \item \textbf{Reproducibility as a design constraint}
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:repro}). Every table and figure is generated by a
  versioned script from versioned evidence files; the figure generators
  re-derive and assert their own claims before rendering; a test suite
  re-derives the pre-registered verdicts from the raw evidence on every
  run.
\end{enumerate}

The unifying object is a per-step error-accumulation model,
$e_{h+1} \approx G \cdot e_h \oplus \text{innovation}(\sigma_{\mathrm{step}})$.
Its amplifying limit ($G = e^{\lambda_1 dt} > 1$) yields the chaotic
horizon law and the twin protocol; its neutral limit ($G \to 1$) yields
the power law validated on the grid. The instrument routes between these
regimes with a measured profile of the series (Sec.~\ref{sec:instrument}),
because chaos diagnostics applied to non-chaotic data produce numbers that
look precise and mean nothing; this failure mode is documented and gated.

The paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} positions the
work. Section~\ref{sec:framework} defines the horizon labels and the two
bounds, with the censoring and certification results.
Section~\ref{sec:floor} presents the twin protocol, the floor
measurements, the injection law, the transport to observation noise, and
the generality boundary. Section~\ref{sec:stable} derives the stable-limit
scaling law, and Sec.~\ref{sec:industrial} reports its pre-registered
validation on grid data. Section~\ref{sec:instrument} describes the
regime-gated estimator, Sec.~\ref{sec:limitations} states limitations and
falsifiable next steps, Sec.~\ref{sec:repro} details the reproducibility
chain, and Sec.~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Predictability of chaotic systems}

The sensitivity of forecasts to initial error and the notion of a finite
predictability horizon go back to
\citet{lorenz1963deterministic,lorenz1965predictability,lorenz1969predictability}.
Twin (identical-twin) experiments perturbing a model against itself, and
parametric error-growth models fitted to operational forecasts, are
standard tools in numerical weather prediction
\citep{lorenz1982atmospheric,dalcher1987error,palmer2000predicting}, and
threshold-based ``skill horizons'' are in operational use
\citep{buizza2015forecast}. The distinction between intrinsic and realized
predictability, that is, how much of the loss is the system's and how much
is the model's, was formalized in information-theoretic terms by
\citet{delsole2007predictability}; our floor ratio $\rho$ is a
horizon-based, paired-measurement instantiation of that distinction.
Quantitative predictability theory relates the horizon to the largest
Lyapunov exponent $\lambda_1$, the tolerance, and the initial error
through $H \sim \ln(\tau/e_0)/\lambda_1$ \citep{boffetta2002predictability}.
Finite-size Lyapunov exponents extend this beyond infinitesimal errors and
already imply, for noise-dominated dynamics,
$\lambda(\delta) \propto \delta^{-2}$ and hence a predictability time
growing as $\tau^2$ \citep{aurell1997predictability}; this is the implicit
ancestor of the stable-limit law we fit and scale-predict on real data in
Sec.~\ref{sec:industrial}. Scale dependence of divergence statistics is
likewise the classical tool for distinguishing chaos from noise
\citep{cencini2000chaos,gao2006distinguishing}; our profiler
(Sec.~\ref{sec:instrument}) applies the same logic to the usable-horizon
curve itself. \citet{brisch2019power} also connect a power law to finite
prediction horizons, but through scale-dependent growth rates in
multi-hierarchical chaotic systems, which is the opposite regime from our
diffusive $H(\tau)$ law; we flag the terminological collision to avoid
confusion. Our twin protocol differs from classical identical-twin
experiments in two ways: the twin is the \emph{true} dynamics (not the
model), and it is seeded at the model's own measured one-step error,
window by window, which turns ``distance to the physical floor'' into a
paired, falsifiable measurement with positive controls.

\subsection{Learned forecasters for chaos}

Reservoir computing achieves multi-Lyapunov-time valid prediction on
chaotic benchmarks \citep{pathak2018modelfree,vlachas2020backpropagation};
next-generation reservoir computing (NG-RC) reformulates it as ridge
regression on polynomial features \citep{gauthier2021ngrc}, reporting
valid times of about $5\,\Tlyap$ on Lorenz-63. Large domain-agnostic
models reach roughly two dozen Lyapunov times across many systems, a
regime in which accuracy notably decorrelates from the Lyapunov exponent
\citep{gilpin2023modelscale}; this motivates a floor-normalized metric
over raw $\Tlyap$ counting. Most relevant to us,
\citet{schotz2025machineprecision} push polynomial methods to 35 to
$105\,\Tlyap$ on Lorenz-63 using high-precision arithmetic (against about
$13\,\Tlyap$ prior state of the art) and show that the numerical ground
truth itself has a finite valid time; \citet{hurley2025reservoir}
independently formalize this as a ``valid ground-truth time'' that no
reported valid time can meaningfully exceed. Both recognize the same
ceiling our injection-floor law (Sec.~\ref{sec:injection}) derives and
measures; neither runs a twin of the true dynamics seeded at the model's
own error, and neither emits a floor certificate. Our 18 to $19\,\Tlyap$
therefore exceeds the classic NG-RC and reservoir-computing figures but
not the high-precision record. The contribution is not the raw number but
the measured, pre-registered ratio to the paired floor at matched $e_0$
($\rho \approx 0.83$), a claim none of the record-setting works make and
one that remains meaningful when raw valid times saturate against
numerical precision. In weather models, \citet{selz2023butterfly} show
that learned forecasters can fail to reproduce true small-error growth,
which is why our protocol carries positive controls on the twin's growth
rate; \citet{vonich2025atmospheric} probe predictability limits by
optimizing initial conditions rather than by paired twins. Recent work
certifies per-instance horizons analytically from model Jacobians
\citep{wang2026certified} or characterizes horizon-constrained model
multiplicity \citep{kale2026rashomon}; neither provides finite-sample,
distribution-free coverage, nor a measured floor comparison. Lyapunov
exponents from data are estimated with standard algorithms
\citep{wolf1985determining,rosenstein1993practical,kantz1994robust}; our
benchmark systems are classical
\citep{lorenz1963deterministic,rossler1976equation,mackey1977oscillation}.

\subsection{Conformal prediction}

Split conformal prediction provides finite-sample, distribution-free
coverage under exchangeability
\citep{vovk2005algorithmic,papadopoulos2002inductive,lei2018distributionfree},
with scale-adaptive variants \citep{romano2019cqr}. Time series break
exchangeability; remedies include conformal inference for dependent data
\citep{chernozhukov2018exact}, weighted conformal methods with explicit
coverage-loss bounds \citep{barber2023conformal}, adaptive recalibration
\citep{gibbs2021adaptive}, and ensemble constructions
\citep{xu2021conformal}. Multi-step conformal forecasting calibrates
per-step intervals over a fixed, pre-specified horizon
\citep{stankeviciute2021conformal,sun2024copula}; conformal runtime
verification predicts specification violations within a fixed horizon
\citep{lindemann2023stl}. The closest structural analogue to our
construction is conformalized survival analysis
\citep{candes2023conformalized,gui2024conformalized,sesia2024doubly},
which emits calibrated lower predictive bounds on a right-censored
survival time; conformal intervals have also been applied to machine
remaining-useful-life estimation \citep{javanmardi2023conformal}. We
transplant the lower-predictive-bound structure to a different object,
the time-to-tolerance-violation of a forecaster's own rollout, where the
censoring is administrative (fixed $\Hmax$). We show this censoring is
conservative for a lower bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:censoring}), so the
weighted corrections needed for informative censoring are not required
here; quantile bias under censoring is handled by censored quantile
regression \citep{powell1986censored}. To our knowledge, and after a
dedicated search, no prior work emits a distribution-free, per-instant
lower bound on a forecaster's usable horizon. We state our coverage as an
empirical target under documented serial dependence, quantified with
circular-block-bootstrap lower bounds
\citep{kunsch1989jackknife,politis1992circular}.

\subsection{Forecastability of real series}

Model-free predictability quantification uses permutation-entropy-type
statistics \citep{bandt2002permutation,garland2014model,
pennekamp2019intrinsic}, with energy-domain applications
\citep{karimiarpanahi2023quantifying}; load forecasting has an extensive
probabilistic literature \citep{hong2016probabilistic}. Noise-level
estimation from chaotic series is classical
\citep{schreiber1993determination}; predicting forecast-error scale from
an estimated noise model has a precedent in technology forecasting
\citep{farmer2016predictable}. A strand of the load-forecasting
literature computes positive Lyapunov exponents on load series and
declares them chaotic, deriving horizons from $1/\lambda$
\citep{li2011application,stergiou2021application}; our profiler's
quasi-periodic verdict and the measured rejection of the logarithmic
$H(\tau)$ signature (Sec.~\ref{sec:industrial}) directly contradict this
interpretation. We are not aware of prior work that (i) measures the
horizon-versus-tolerance curve $H(\tau)$ on industrial data and uses its
functional form to discriminate stochastic from chaotic error
accumulation, or (ii) predicts the scale of that curve from an
independent one-step noise estimate; Sec.~\ref{sec:industrial} does both,
pre-registered.

\section{Horizon labels and calibrated lower bounds}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{Setup and horizon labels}

Let $y_t$ be a scalar observable of a dynamical system, sampled at step
$dt$ and standardized on the training split. A forecaster $f$ (any model,
built-in baseline or user-supplied) is rolled out autoregressively from a
window $w$ ending at time $t$, producing $\hat y_{t+1}, \dots,
\hat y_{t+\Hmax}$.

\begin{definition}[Right-censored horizon label]
\label{def:label}
Given tolerance $\tau > 0$ and persistence $K \ge 1$, the horizon label of
window $w$ is
\begin{multline}
\Hw = \min\{h \ge 1 : |\hat y_{t+j} - y_{t+j}| > \tau \\
\text{for } j = h, \dots, h+K-1\},
\end{multline}
and $\Hw = \Hmax$ (right-censored) if no such $h \le \Hmax$ exists. We use
$K=2$ and the attractor-scale tolerance $\tau = 0.4\,\sigma$ of the
standardized series (the ``valid time'' convention) throughout.
\end{definition}

$\Hmax$ is resolved automatically in Lyapunov times,
$\Hmax = \max\!\big(3\,\Tlyap,\ 1.2\,\ln(\tau/e_0)/\lambda_1\big)/dt$
steps, clamped to $[30, 400]$ and to the data budget. An identification guard requires
the saturation rate $p_{\mathrm{sat}} = \mathbb{P}(\Hw = \Hmax)$ to
satisfy $p_{\mathrm{sat}} \le 1-\alpha$; otherwise the target quantile
lies in the censored region and the label is flagged unidentified.

\subsection{The conformal lower bound}
\label{sec:conformal}

Let $\hat q_\alpha(x)$ be a learned conditional $\alpha$-quantile of the
label of Definition~\ref{def:label} given per-window features $x$, and
$\hat s(x) > 0$ a learned scale. On a calibration set of $n$ windows we
compute signed, scale-normalized nonconformity scores
$s_i = (\hat q_\alpha(x_i) - \Hw^{(i)})/\hat s(x_i)$ and set $c$ to the
$\lceil (n+1)(1-\alpha) \rceil$-th order statistic of $\{s_i\}$. The
emitted bound is
\begin{equation}
L(x) \;=\; \hat q_\alpha(x) \;-\; c\,\hat s(x).
\label{eq:bound}
\end{equation}
Under exchangeability of calibration and test windows, standard split
conformal arguments give $\mathbb{P}(\Hw \ge L(x)) \ge 1-\alpha$ in finite
samples. Overlapping rollout windows are serially dependent, so
exchangeability does not hold exactly; we treat the guarantee as an
empirical target under documented dependence and support it in three
ways. (i)~Thinning: subsampling the calibration set at stride 12 leaves
coverage unchanged ($0.941$ at $n_{\mathrm{cal}} = 1200$ and at $116$)
before small-$n$ collapse ($0.73$ at $n = 29$); correlated windows are
redundant, not biasing. (ii)~Shift remedy: the residual one-point deficit
on fast-growing regimes is a calibration-to-test shift along the
trajectory; calibrating at $\alpha_{\mathrm{cal}} = \alpha - 0.015$
restores delivery ($0.951$, worst seed $0.947$, tightness cost below
1\%). This remedy was fixed once, on simulated data, before any contact
with the real datasets of Sec.~\ref{sec:industrial}.
(iii)~Dependence-aware reporting: measured coverages are accompanied by
95\% circular-block-bootstrap lower bounds \citep{politis1992circular}.
We also document that percentile bootstrap lower bounds are optimistic
under very strong dependence (undercoverage $0.815$ vs.\ nominal $0.95$
at AR(1) $\varphi = 0.9$, irrespective of block length; this is a
studentization issue and remains open).

\subsection{Censoring is conservative for a lower bound}

\begin{proposition}[Censoring conservativity]
\label{prop:censoring}
Let $\Hw^{\mathrm{obs}} = \min(\Hw, \Hmax)$ and let $L^{\mathrm{obs}}$ be
the bound of Eq.~\eqref{eq:bound} calibrated on censored labels. Then,
under the exchangeability assumptions of the uncensored procedure,
$\mathbb{P}(\Hw \ge L^{\mathrm{obs}}(x)) \ge 1-\alpha$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{myproof}
Since $\Hw^{\mathrm{obs}} \le \Hw$ pointwise, every signed score satisfies
$s_i^{\mathrm{obs}} = (\hat q_\alpha(x_i) - \Hw^{\mathrm{obs},(i)})/\hat
s(x_i) \ge s_i$. The calibrated margin $c$ is a fixed upper order
statistic of the scores, hence $c^{\mathrm{obs}} \ge c$, hence
$L^{\mathrm{obs}}(x) \le L(x)$ pointwise. Therefore $\{\Hw \ge L(x)\}
\subseteq \{\Hw \ge L^{\mathrm{obs}}(x)\}$ and the uncensored guarantee
transfers. Censoring costs tightness, never coverage.
\end{myproof}

When censoring bites the quantile region, the quantile model itself is
biased; a censored (Powell-type) pinball loss \citep{powell1986censored}
with the capped score $\min(\hat q, \Hmax) - \Hw^{\mathrm{obs}}$ applied
consistently to score and bound reduces the quantile bias by 59\% in our
ablations while preserving coverage ($0.916$ to $0.999$ across settings).

\subsection{A certified per-window bound}

The statistical bound covers a fraction $1-\alpha$ of windows. A
complementary bound covers every window, at the price of looseness.

\begin{proposition}[Certified horizon]
\label{prop:certified}
In the delay-embedding space with the sup norm, let the one-step map
$F(x) = (x_2, \dots, x_d, f(x))$ satisfy $\|F(x) - F(y)\|_\infty \le
G\|x-y\|_\infty$ with $G = \max(1, L_f^\infty)$, where $L_f^\infty$ is a
Lipschitz bound for $f$ (exact $\|w\|_1$ for linear $f$; product of
row-sum operator norms for an MLP). If the one-step residual is bounded
by $\delta$ on the trajectory, then the rollout error obeys
$e_h \le \delta\,(G^h - 1)/(G - 1)$, and
\begin{equation}
h_{\mathrm{cert}} \;=\; \min\{h : \delta\,(G^h - 1)/(G-1) \ge \tau\}
\end{equation}
satisfies $\Hw \ge h_{\mathrm{cert}}$ for every window on which the
residual bound holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{myproof}
By the triangle inequality, $e_{h+1} \le G e_h + \delta$; unrolling the
recursion from $e_0 = 0$ gives the geometric bound, and $\Hw$ cannot
occur before the bound first reaches $\tau$.
\end{myproof}

The weak link is $\delta$, taken as the empirical supremum of calibration
residuals; the bound is certified modulo the validity of $\delta$ on the
attractor. Measured: 0 violations across 2000 windows (Lorenz/R\"ossler
$\times$ linear/MLP), including with $\delta \times 1.5$ and with
$\delta$ estimated on a disjoint segment. Utility is limited by the
looseness of $G$ (e.g.\ $\|w\|_1 \approx 5.7$ against true growth
$e^{\lambda_1 dt} \approx 1.009$):
$h_{\mathrm{cert}}/\mathrm{med}(\Hw) \in [0.09, 0.20]$. We export it as a
diagnostic, not as the operational bound.

\section{The physical floor, measured}
\label{sec:floor}

Claiming that a model has reached the limit of predictability requires an
operational, falsifiable definition of the limit. Ours is measured, not
postulated.

\subsection{Paired twin protocol}
\label{sec:twins}

For each test window $w$ (Lorenz-63, $dt = 0.01$, $\tau = 0.4\sigma$,
$K = 2$, 250 windows per run):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{One-shot twin.} Integrate the \emph{true} dynamics from
  the exact full state at the window start, perturbed by an error of
  magnitude $e_{0,w}$ equal to the model's own one-step error on that
  window (paired per window, random direction). Its horizon
  $H_{\mathrm{twin}}$, computed with the same observable, the same
  $\tau$, and the same $K$, is the physical floor at that error level: no
  predictor can systematically beat the true dynamics at equal initial
  error.
  \item \textbf{Injection twin.} A step-by-step forecaster re-injects an
  error of size $\sim e_0$ at every step. The second twin adds isotropic
  noise of size $e_0$ after each step of the true dynamics: the fair
  floor for any sequential predictor.
  \item \textbf{Pre-registered criterion.} The floor is declared reached
  if and only if $\mathrm{med}_w\,\rho_w \ge 0.8$, where $\rho_w =
  H_{\mathrm{model},w}/H_{\mathrm{twin},w}$.
  \item \textbf{Positive controls.} The one-shot twin's effective growth
  rate must equal $\lambda_1$: measured $R_{\mathrm{twin}} =
  \Lambda_\eff/\lambda_1 \in \{0.96, 0.97, 1.02, 1.03\}$ across all runs.
  This control matters because learned forecasters can fail to reproduce
  true small-error growth \citep{selz2023butterfly}; an apparatus that
  claims to measure error growth must first demonstrate that it recovers
  $\lambda_1$ on the true dynamics. Both members of each twin pair use
  the same integrator (fixed-step RK4, $h = dt/4$, mutual distance), so
  integration error is common-mode. An anti-censoring guard invalidates
  any verdict whose twin horizons are censored at $\Hmax$ (this guard
  vetoed an over-optimistic first R\"ossler verdict).
\end{itemize}

The model under test is NG-RC \citep{gauthier2021ngrc}: ridge regression
on polynomial monomials of the full state, degree 3 to 6 (84 coefficients
at degree 6), with no knowledge of the equations.

Table~\ref{tab:transition} shows the measured transition: as the model
family strengthens (linear to MLP to NG-RC degree 3 through 6; observable
to full state), $R$ falls from 41 to 1.15 and $\rho$ rises to the
pre-registered threshold. Table~\ref{tab:verdicts} gives the verdicts: on
two independent seeds, the degree-6 NG-RC reaches the physical floor
under both twin definitions, with about 18 to 19 Lyapunov times of valid
prediction and error growing at $1.15$ to $1.21$ times the Lyapunov rate.
In the paired-twin sense, the model has exhausted the predictive content
of the system to within 14 to 17\%.

A remark on reading the 18 to $19\,\Tlyap$ figure: it is obtained under
idealized conditions (clean data, full state, $e_0 \approx 2\times
10^{-10}\sigma$). It exceeds the classic NG-RC and reservoir-computing
valid times (about $5\,\Tlyap$, \citealp{gauthier2021ngrc,
pathak2018modelfree}) but is below the high-precision-arithmetic record
\citep{schotz2025machineprecision}. The contribution is not the raw
horizon but the measured ratio to the paired floor at matched $e_0$, a
quantity that is invariant to how favorable the conditions are, comes
with positive controls, was pre-registered, and remains meaningful in
the regime where raw valid times saturate against numerical precision.

\subsection{The injection-floor law}
\label{sec:injection}

A step-by-step predictor with one-step error $e_0$ effectively injects
$e_0$ at every step; each injection is then amplified at rate
$e^{\lambda_1 dt}$ per step. Summing the geometric series,
$e_h \approx e_0 \sum_{k=0}^{h-1} e^{\lambda_1 dt\,k} \approx
\frac{e_0}{\lambda_1 dt}\, e^{\lambda_1 dt\,h}$ for
$\lambda_1 dt\, h \gg 1$; setting $e_h = \tau$,
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{inj}} \;\approx\; \frac{\ln\!\big(\tau \lambda_1\,dt / e_0\big)}
{\lambda_1\,dt}.
\label{eq:injection}
\end{equation}
Measured against Eq.~\eqref{eq:injection}, the injection twin's horizon
matches to a factor 1.12 to 1.21 across all Lorenz runs and 1.12 on
R\"ossler, a systematic bias in the expected direction (isotropic noise
projects only partially onto the unstable direction; the geometric $O(1)$
factor has not been derived). Equation~\eqref{eq:injection} implies that
the irreducible cost of step-by-step prediction relative to the one-shot
floor is $\ln(1/(\lambda_1 dt))/\ln(\tau/e_0)$, vanishing
logarithmically as $e_0 \to 0$; the degree progression verifies this
($\rho$: $0.49 \to 0.68 \to 0.83$). This is the same ceiling recently
recognized from the numerical side as the finite valid time of the ground
truth itself \citep{schotz2025machineprecision,hurley2025reservoir}, here
derived as a law in $(e_0, \lambda_1 dt, \tau)$ and measured against a
physically launched twin.

\subsection{Transport to observation noise}
\label{sec:noise}

Twins require the true dynamics and are impossible on real data. The
bridge is the noise-floor law: under observation noise $\sigma$, the
reachable horizon is $H_{\mathrm{reach}} \approx
\ln(\tau/\sigma)/(\lambda_1 dt)$. Three pre-registered claims were tested
on Lorenz with known synthetic noise
$\sigma \in \{10^{-3}, 10^{-2}, 3\times 10^{-2}\}\,\sigma_{\mathrm{std}}$.
(C1) The local-linear residual estimator $\hat\sigma$ (median absolute
deviation, robust; cf.\ noise-level determination for chaotic series,
\citealp{schreiber1993determination}) recovers $\sigma$ within a factor
2: measured ratios $1.84, 1.84, 1.59$, a documented upward bias of
curvature origin, hence conservative for a reachable-horizon estimate.
(C2) The measured floor matches the law within $[0.8, 1.3]$: measured
$0.99, 0.91, 0.88$. (C3) The model's $\rho$ against the noisy floor
behaves as a valid bound: measured $0.967$ (floor reached), $0.787$, and
$0.633$ (near-floor). This yields the deployable diagnostic
$\texttt{margin\_real} = H_{\mathrm{reach}}/\mathrm{med}(\Hw)$, a
quantified answer to the practical question of whether improving the
model is worthwhile or the residual gap is noise-irreducible.

\section{The stable limit: a different horizon law}
\label{sec:stable}

The error-accumulation model $e_{h+1} \approx G\,e_h \oplus
\mathrm{innovation}(\sigma_{\mathrm{step}})$ has two limits with
incompatible signatures.

In the chaotic limit ($G = e^{\lambda_1 dt} > 1$, validated by twins in
Sec.~\ref{sec:floor}), amplification dominates,
$e_h \approx e_0 e^{\lambda_1 dt\,h}$, hence
\begin{equation}
H(\tau) \;=\; \frac{\ln(\tau/e_0)}{\lambda_1\,dt},
\label{eq:chaoticlaw}
\end{equation}
so that $H$ is linear in $\ln\tau$.

In the stable or neutral limit ($\lambda \to 0$, the profiler's
prediction for a power grid), there is no exponential amplification;
per-step innovations accumulate in variance,
$e_h^2 \approx e_0^2 + h\,\sigma_{\mathrm{step}}^2$, hence for
$\tau \gg e_0$
\begin{equation}
H(\tau) \;\approx\; \big(\tau/\sigma_\eff\big)^{s}, \qquad
s \in [1,2],
\label{eq:powerlaw}
\end{equation}
with $s = 2$ for pure diffusion and $s < 2$ for damped or anticorrelated
innovations, so that $\ln H$ is linear in $\ln\tau$. Crucially, the scale
is predicted with no free parameter: $\sigma_\eff$ extracted from the
$H(\tau)$ fit must coincide, within the estimator's documented bias band,
with the one-step residual $\hat\sigma$ measured independently by the
local-linear estimator of Sec.~\ref{sec:noise}. The $s = 2$ endpoint is
the horizon-curve counterpart of the FSLE result
$\lambda(\delta) \propto \delta^{-2}$ for noise-dominated dynamics
\citep{aurell1997predictability}.

For load data, mean reversion toward the daily profile damps innovations,
which is why $s < 2$ is expected. The two functional forms (logarithmic
vs.\ power) are distinguishable by fit on a modest $\tau$ grid, giving a
falsifiable discrimination of the error-accumulation regime on real data.

\section{Pre-registered industrial validation}
\label{sec:industrial}

\subsection{Protocol}

Data: hourly load from two independent balancing regions of the U.S.\
Eastern Interconnection, AEP (American Electric Power) and PJM-East
(PJME), covering the last three years of each record, sorted and
DST-deduplicated. AEP is evaluated on two consecutive sub-periods,
denoted p1 and p2, to test out-of-period replication; PJME is evaluated
on its full span. All four claims and every threshold below were written
in the study script's docstring before any run (pre-registration); the
raw evidence is a versioned CSV pinned by the test suite. Forecasters: a
deliberately simple AR-linear model (window 25\,h) and a seasonal-naive
rule (24-hour persistence); tolerance grid
$\tau \in \{0.2, 0.3, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8\}\,\sigma$; 400 test windows for the
$H(\tau)$ sweep, zero censoring; conformal target $0.90$ with the
$\alpha$-remedy $\alpha_{\mathrm{cal}} = 0.085$ fixed on Lorenz
beforehand (Sec.~\ref{sec:conformal}). Pre-registered acceptance bands:
regime = quasi-periodic (V1); coverage $\ge 0.88$ with bootstrap lower
bound $\ge 0.85$ on all six calibrations (V2); slope $s \in [1.2, 2.8]$,
power law beating the chaotic signature, and
$\sigma_\eff/\hat\sigma \in [1/3, 3]$, the factor-3 band encoding the
estimator's documented upward bias (V3); the learned model's median $L$
above the naive rule's in both periods, out-of-period stability ratio in
$[0.6, 1.4]$, and $\mathrm{Spearman}(L, H) > 0.3$ (V4).

\subsection{What this validates, and what it does not}

Established: (i) the error-accumulation framework's stable limit
describes real industrial data at $R^2 \approx 0.99$ with its scale
predicted by an independent measurement, which is the theory leaving the
simulator; (ii) the conformal guarantee holds in the bootstrap sense on
dependent real data, six of six times, with a remedy fixed in advance;
(iii) the profiler, the bound, and the decision behave and replicate as
specified. Not established: the two grids share a continent (climatic
correlation is possible); the simple forecaster understates
state-of-the-art absolute horizons (the values of $L$, not the laws,
would change with a stronger model); and the slope $s$ is measured within
its predicted band, not finely predicted. A model of innovation
autocorrelation would be required for the latter; this is our next
falsifiable question.

\section{The instrument: regime-gated horizon estimation}
\label{sec:instrument}

The components above are packaged as an estimator with a
bring-your-own-forecaster interface (any callable or \texttt{.predict}).
Two design decisions matter scientifically.

\paragraph{Regime gating.} Chaos diagnostics applied outside their regime
produce numbers that look precise and mean nothing. On a quasi-periodic
photoplethysmography series (BIDMC), $\lambda \approx 0$ made the raw
diagnostic $R = \Lambda_\eff/\lambda_1$ explode to about 196, numerically
correct and physically meaningless. A profiler therefore classifies each
series (stochastic, chaotic, quasi-periodic, or regular) from measured
signals: noise level $\hat\sigma$, a periodicity score, and a
resolved-growth criterion. It then routes: $R$ and floor diagnostics are
only emitted in the chaotic regime; $L(x)$ and $\texttt{margin\_real}$
are emitted everywhere.

\paragraph{The resolved-growth criterion.} Declaring a series chaotic
requires both (i) a growth product $\lambda \times \mathrm{window} \ge 1$
(the divergence must be resolvable within the analysis window; an
absolute $\lambda$ threshold would misclassify slow chaos such as Lorenz
at small $dt$) and (ii) structure beyond persistence, $\hat\sigma /
\sigma_{\mathrm{pers}} < 0.5$. The second gate exists because a pure
random walk exhibits diffusive divergence that fools log-linear
divergence fits (fit quality indistinguishable: $R^2$ $0.8674$ vs.\
$0.8676$ on matched chaotic data); persistence-relative structure
separates them. This is the classical chaos-versus-noise difficulty
\citep{cencini2000chaos,gao2006distinguishing} encountered as a concrete
engineering failure mode, and gated accordingly.

For completeness, the calibrated-bound benchmark across four classical
systems (linear and MLP forecasters, five seeds, $\alpha = 0.05$;
Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}) delivers median coverage $0.93$ to $0.99$
with one documented negative result: on R\"ossler with an MLP, the test
split spans too few Lyapunov times (about 4) for stable coverage
estimation (worst seed $0.700$). This sets a concrete data-budget
requirement, $n_{\mathrm{test}} \gtrsim$ tens of $\Tlyap$, for horizon
studies on slow chaotic systems.

\section{Limitations and falsifiable next steps}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Floor result.} Reached on Lorenz only, under clean data
  and full-state access; observable-only access caps at $R \approx 7$
  (delay embedding degrades the learned Jacobian). Open question: can
  state reconstruction close the gap from the observable alone?
  \item \textbf{Model family.} The Mackey--Glass failure predicts that a
  rational or kernel family captures the Hill Jacobian and approaches its
  floor; this is untested.
  \item \textbf{Statistical guarantee.} Coverage under serial dependence
  is an empirical target supported by thinning, bootstrap lower bounds,
  and a pre-fixed $\alpha$-margin, not a finite-sample theorem;
  percentile bootstrap lower bounds are themselves optimistic under very
  strong dependence (studentization open); and deployment under
  distribution drift would require shift-aware recalibration
  \citep{tibshirani2019covariate,gibbs2021adaptive}.
  \item \textbf{Industrial validation.} Two grids, one continent, one
  operator family; a European or Asian grid transfer is the designed next
  test. The slope $s$ is measured within $[1,2]$, not finely predicted.
  \item \textbf{Conventions.} $\tau = 0.4\sigma$ and $K = 2$ are fixed
  conventions ($\tau$-robustness was swept only on the industrial grids);
  the $0.8$ floor threshold is pre-registered but conventional, and the
  seed-level IQRs straddle it.
\end{itemize}

\section{Reproducibility and data availability}
\label{sec:repro}

Every number in this paper is generated by a versioned script; no value
is hand-copied. The evidence chain has three layers: (i) study scripts
with pre-registered criteria in their docstrings write raw evidence CSV
files; (ii) figure generators re-derive every claim from the evidence and
assert it before rendering, so a figure whose checks fail is not
generated; (iii) the test suite (235 tests) re-derives the pre-registered
verdicts from the versioned evidence on every run, so silent drift
between claims and evidence fails continuous integration. Physical
constants ($\lambda_1$ of each benchmark system) are pinned to literature
values by blocking tests; the Mackey--Glass generator is a true
delay-differential-equation integrator (method of steps); Lyapunov
estimation follows \citet{rosenstein1993practical} with automated Theiler
windows and linear regions. Appendix~\ref{app:repromap} maps each claim
of this paper to its generating script, evidence file, and pinning test.
The complete repository (scripts, evidence files, and this manuscript) is
public at \url{https://github.com/DEX-zha/ARSAC-Horizon-Experiment}. The
load data are the public PJM hourly records; the chaotic benchmarks are
generated by the repository's own integrators.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to give a forecast an expiry date, and to know when that date
is set by the physics rather than by the model. The resulting instrument
emits, for any forecaster, a per-instant lower bound $L(x)$ on the usable
horizon with a stated coverage target, a certified worst-case companion,
and regime-gated diagnostics that say how far the model sits from the
reachable floor.

Three results support it. First, the bound itself: right-censoring is
provably conservative, coverage behavior under serial dependence is
measured rather than assumed, and the one remedy it needs was fixed on
simulated data before touching any real series. Second, the floor: a
paired twin protocol with pre-registered thresholds and positive controls
showed that a degree-6 NG-RC reaches the physical predictability floor of
Lorenz-63 on two seeds, quantified the irreducible cost of step-by-step
prediction through a derived and verified injection law, and located the
generality boundary in the model family's ability to represent the
Jacobian. Third, the transport to the real world: in the stable limit the
same framework predicts a power-law horizon curve whose scale is fixed by
an independent noise measurement, and a 17-of-17 pre-registered
validation on two independent power grids confirmed the law, the scale,
the coverage, and the replication of the operational decision.

The designed next tests are explicit: a European or Asian grid transfer
for the scaling law, a rational or kernel model family against the
Mackey--Glass boundary, the observable-only floor, and a studentized
bootstrap for the coverage lower bounds. Each is falsifiable with the
tools already in the repository.
